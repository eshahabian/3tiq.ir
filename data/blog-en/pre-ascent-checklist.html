Most mountaineering incidents start with small oversights: no weather check, a forgotten headlamp, or nobody knowing where you went. A simple pre-ascent checklist reduces risk and lets you focus on enjoying the mountain.

Print this list or save it on your phone — useful for day hikes, multi-day trips, and short training walks near the city.

10 essentials

1. Weather forecast (3 sources) Check at least two websites and one local app. Watch wind speed at altitude, precipitation chance, and night temperature. If all three warn you, postpone the climb.
2. Route & map Mark the route on an offline or paper map. Note the trailhead, shelters, water sources, and emergency descent. If it is your first time, go with someone who has done it before.
3. Ten essential items Proper boots, layered clothing, rain shell, hat, gloves, sunglasses, headlamp + spare batteries, whistle, first-aid kit, and enough food/water for 24 hours beyond your plan.
4. Team & roles Match group pace to the slowest member. Assign navigation and timekeeping. Agree emergency signals before you start (e.g. three whistle blasts).
5. Tell someone Inform one person in town: where you are going, with whom, expected return time, and what to do if they hear nothing. Keep mountain emergency numbers on your phone.
6. Physical condition A cold, poor sleep, or joint pain is enough reason to cancel. Tell the team about personal medications and chronic conditions.
7. Food & water Rule of thumb: at least 1.5 litres of water per 3 hours of activity at moderate altitude. Dry bread, nuts, dried fruit, and chocolate give quick energy. Avoid heavy meals at the start.
8. Realistic timing Plan for sunrise and set a turn-around time — if you have not reached the summit by then, go back. An unplanned night on the mountain is more dangerous than missing the top.
9. Permits & regulations Some areas (e.g. Damavand in peak season) require shelter booking or permits. Check 3tiq.ir or official sources before you leave.
10. “Turn back” mindset The summit will always be there. If something feels wrong — cold, headache, whiteout — turn back without shame. That is professionalism, not weakness.
