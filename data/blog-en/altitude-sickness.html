Acclimatization

Altitude sickness (AMS) occurs when the body cannot adapt fast enough to lower oxygen at elevation. Fitness does not protect you — fit athletes can suffer more than beginners.

Types of altitude illness

1. Acute Mountain Sickness (AMS)

Most common form. Usually appears 6–12 hours after gaining altitude.

Symptoms: headache, fatigue, loss of appetite, nausea, dizziness, poor sleep

2. High Altitude Pulmonary Edema (HAPE)

Fluid in the lungs — medical emergency.

Symptoms: breathlessness at rest, dry or pink frothy cough, blue lips

3. High Altitude Cerebral Edema (HACE)

Rare but fatal — fluid in the brain.

Symptoms: severe headache unresponsive to medication, ataxia, confusion, loss of consciousness

⚠️ Golden rule
If HAPE or HACE is suspected, descend immediately. Even 300–500 m down can save a life. No summit is worth your life.

Prevention

  • Climb high, sleep low
  • Max 300–500 m vertical gain per day above 3,000 m
  • Extra rest day for every 1,000 m gained
  • 2–3 days at 2,500–3,000 m before the climb
  • Drink 3–4 litres daily; eat carbohydrates; avoid alcohol and sedatives

Medication

Acetazolamide (Diamox) is the most common preventive drug — start 24 hours before ascent. Doctor's prescription only.

Field treatment

  • Mild AMS: rest at same altitude, ibuprofen for headache
  • Moderate/severe AMS: descend 300–500 m immediately
  • HAPE/HACE: emergency descent, call for help, oxygen if available
✅ Practical summary
Go slow, drink plenty, take symptoms seriously, descend when in doubt. Experience does not prevent AMS — smart prevention does.
